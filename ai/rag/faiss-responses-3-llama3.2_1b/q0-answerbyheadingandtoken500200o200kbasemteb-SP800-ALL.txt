How does NIST define the term 'least privilege', and why is it important?

4.912539605051279

The term "least privilege" is defined in the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Security Framework, specifically in SP800-53 Rev. 5****. According to this document, the principle of least privilege states that each system component should be allocated sufficient privileges to accomplish its specified functions but no more.

In essence, the least privilege principle limits the scope of a component's actions, which has two desirable effects: minimizing the security impact of failures, corruption, or misuse, and simplifying the security analysis of components. This means that organizations must apply this principle in all aspects of their secure system design to create an effective defense.

The importance of the least privilege principle lies in its ability to reduce the attack surface of a system and limit the potential damage caused by unauthorized access or actions. By allocating sufficient privileges, systems can minimize security risks and ensure that users and components are granted only the necessary permissions to perform their intended functions.

It's worth noting that the least privilege principle is also reflected in other NIST security frameworks, such as SP800-171 Rev. 3****, which discusses the concept of "least functionality" and its role in reducing attack surfaces.
