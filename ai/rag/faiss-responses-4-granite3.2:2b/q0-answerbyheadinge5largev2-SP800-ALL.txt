In accordance with Special Publication 800-30 (SP800-30 Rev. 1), risk is defined as a function of two key components:

1. **Likelihood** - This refers to the probability that a specific threat event will occur. It represents how likely an adverse circumstance or event is to happen within a given timeframe.
2. **Potential Adverse Impacts** - This component describes the consequences that could result if the threat event were to transpire. These impacts can vary widely, extending from financial loss and damage to reputation to disruptions in mission-critical functions or compromise of sensitive data. They encompass both tangible (e.g., financial loss) and intangible elements (e.g., reputational harm), thus providing a comprehensive view of potential threats to an organization's operations, assets, individuals, other organizations, and national security interests.

Hence, risk is essentially the product of these two factors: likelihood multiplied by potential adverse impacts. This definition underscores that assessing risks involves quantifying both the probability of unwanted events happening and their expected detrimental effects on a wide array of organizational components.

How does NIST define the term 'least privilege', and why is it important?

6.2291981927119195

NIST defines 'least privilege' as an essential security principle that allocates to each system component or user (referred to as a "trusted subject") only the minimum permissions required for them to carry out their designated tasks, but no more. This concept is crucial for several reasons when applied in cybersecurity:

1. **Minimized Impact of Failure**: If a system component or user with limited privileges experiences failure, corruption, or malicious misuse, the potential damage to other parts of the system (or broader network) can be significantly reduced since their scope is confined. This principle thus helps in limiting the blast radius of security incidents.

2. **Simplified Security Analysis**: By adhering strictly to least privilege, the overall complexity of assessing and maintaining system security is diminished. The security analyst's task becomes more manageable as they only need to scrutinize and secure a fraction of the system's resources rather than an entire set of potentially unrestricted functionalities.

3. **Enhanced System Hardness**: Implementing least privilege in hardware, software, or through policies (as emphasized in SP800-53 Rev. 5) makes systems more resilient against known attacks and vulnerabilities. Attackers are less likely to find paths into sensitive areas due to the constrained permissions.

4. **Vulnerability Reduction Over Time**: The principle encourages acquisition of software or partnering with vendors who consistently develop secure products, thereby reducing long-term vulnerabilities inherent in systems using such components over time.

5. **Policy Enforcement and Compliance**: Adherence to least privilege aids organizations in fulfilling compliance requirements stipulated by regulations like FISMA (as reflected in SP800-123), ensuring they manage cybersecurity effectively while protecting critical information assets.

In summary, the NIST's definition of 'least privilege' as an allocation of necessary permissions only to trusted subjects is a key strategy for robust security management because it helps in minimizing both the impact of compromises and the complexity of maintaining secure systems. This approach aligns with broader cybersecurity goals to enhance resilience, reduce attack surfaces, and ensure compliance with various regulations.
