According to SP 800-30, what is the definition of 'risk'?

1.1122393440455198

In the context of NIST Special Publication (SP) 800-30, "Risk Assessment Techniques," which focuses on risk management for information security, a risk is defined as:

"A potentially adverse event that, if it occurs, would have a negative impact on an organization's objectives."

This definition emphasizes three key elements of risk:

1. **Potentiality**: The threat (the thing that could cause harm) must exist or be present in the system or environment under consideration.
2. **Impact**: This refers to the consequences if the adverse event occurs, which can include financial loss, damage to reputation, violation of regulations, or disruption of services. Impact is often quantified in terms of likelihood and severity (for example, low likelihood of minor impact).
3. **Objectives**: These are the goals that the organization aims to achieve with its systems, information, or assets. Risks are considered adverse events when they threaten these objectives.

In simpler terms, a risk is an event or set of circumstances that might negatively affect something valuable (the objective) due to a potential hazard (threat). The term 'risk' in this context helps organizations understand and manage threats effectively to protect their interests.

What are the steps in the risk assessment process dexcribed in SP 800-30?

3.639067320153117

The NIST Special Publication (SP) 800-30, "Evaluation of Contingency Plans," outlines a comprehensive risk assessment process for cybersecurity and business continuity planning. Here are the key steps in the risk assessment process described in SP 800-30:

1. **Define the Scope**: Clearly outline the systems, data, assets, or processes to be evaluated within the organization's critical infrastructure. This includes identifying all relevant components and their interdependencies.

2. **Identify Threats and Vulnerabilities**:
   - **Threat Identification**: Gather and categorize potential adversaries (both internal and external) who might exploit vulnerabilities to compromise the system or disrupt operations. Include known threat agents, as well as hypothetical ones based on intelligence and understanding of industry threats.
   - **Vulnerability Assessment**: Identify weaknesses in systems, software, hardware, people, policies, procedures, third-party services, physical security, and other factors that could be exploited by the identified threats. Use methods such as penetration testing, vulnerability scanning, or reviews of documentation to find these vulnerabilities.

3. **Determine Risks**:
   - **Threat-Vulnerability Matrix (TVM) Analysis**: Combine threat and vulnerability information from steps 2a and 2b into a table or matrix format. This helps visualize the probability of each threat exploiting a particular vulnerability, given its current level of awareness by defenders. Calculate risk scores based on these probabilities and their consequences. Commonly used risk assessment methodologies include:
     - *Simple Risk Matrix*: Probability of Threat x Consequence
     - *Risk Scoring Models* (e.g., COBIT, NIST SP 800-34): Incorporate additional factors like assets' value or business impact to calculate a risk score.
   - **Likelihood and Impact Assessment**: Estimate the likelihood of each threat exploiting vulnerabilities under normal conditions and in adverse conditions (if applicable). Also, assess the potential consequences of successful exploitation, which can include data loss, system unavailability, reputational damage, or operational disruption.

4. **Prioritize Risks**: Rank risks based on their calculated scores. This prioritization helps organizations focus resources on addressing the most significant threats first. Common methods for risk ranking include:
   - *Risk Matrix (as in TVM analysis)*
   - *Risk Scoring Models* mentioned above
   - *Expected Monetary Value (EMV) or Expected Loss (EL)* calculations

5. **Develop Risk Mitigation Strategies**: For each prioritized risk, design and document appropriate countermeasures to reduce the likelihood of threat occurrence or diminish its potential impacts. Consider the following types of controls:
   - Preventive Controls (to block threats)
   - Detective Controls (to identify threats early)
   - Corrective Actions (to limit or contain damage if a threat occurs)

6. **Implement and Monitor Risk Mitigation Strategies**: Execute plans to implement the chosen countermeasures, ensuring they are properly configured, tested, and operational. Establish a process for ongoing monitoring of these controls to maintain their effectiveness over time, including regular testing and updating as conditions change.

7. **Review and Update Risk Assessment**: Periodically revisit the risk assessment due to changes in threats, vulnerabilities, business objectives, or other factors. This iterative process keeps the organization's risk management effective and adaptable.

8. **Evaluate Contingency Plans** (if applicable): Using a portion of the findings from the risk assessment, develop, maintain, and regularly test contingency plans that support recovery efforts in response to high-priority risks. These plans should outline steps to mitigate or minimize the impacts when incidents occur and help restore normal operations as quickly as possible.

By following these detailed steps in SP 800-30, organizations can systematically manage cybersecurity and business continuity risks, enhancing their preparedness and resilience against potential threats.

Describe the phases of the risk management framework (RMF) as per SP 800-37.

3.4994517196901143

The Risk Management Framework (RMF), as defined in Special Publication 800-37 by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), is a structured, iterative process for managing information risk. It's primarily used to secure federal information systems and organizations. Here are its key phases:

1. **Risk Assessment (RA) - Plan Phase:**
   - **Define Information System Assessable Entity (ISAE):** Identify the system, data, or component that will be subjected to RMF processes. This could be an entire network, a software application, or specific hardware components.
   - **Identify Information System Threats and Vulnerabilities (ITVs):** List all potential threats to your ISAE (e.g., hacking attempts, natural disasters) and their corresponding vulnerabilities (weaknesses that allow these threats to exploit).
   - **Determine Impact of Threats:** Estimate the severity of harm or loss if a threat were to occur. This typically involves a qualitative assessment on a 1-5 scale (e.g., Minor, Moderate, Major, Destructive, Catastrophic).
   - **Select Security Controls:** From NIST's Special Publication 800-53, choose appropriate security controls based on the risk category and impact level you've determined for your ISAE. The publication provides a matrix to assist with this selection.

2. **Risk Selection (RS) - Assess Phase:**
   - **Assess Security Controls:** For each select control, evaluate its effectiveness in mitigating identified threats and reducing the potential impact of those threats. This usually involves testing or analysis.
   - **Determine Implementation Status:** Identify whether each selected security control is implemented as planned within your ISAE.

3. **Risk Treatment (RT) - Plan Phase:**

   - **Plan Risk Treatment Actions:** For each uncontrolled or underperforming security control, plan specific actions to remediate the issue and ensure it meets the required effectiveness level.
   - **Implement Risk Treatment Actions:** Execute the planned measures to treat identified risks. This could include updating configurations, patching software, enhancing access controls, or training staff on new security practices.

4. **Risk Assessment (RA) - Measure Phase:**
   - **Verify Implementation of Security Controls and Treatments:** Confirm that all intended risk treatment actions are implemented correctly within your ISAE. This could involve documentation reviews, testing, or other forms of verification.
   - **Evaluate Control Effectiveness:** Assess whether the implemented controls have achieved the desired level of effectiveness in mitigating threats and reducing impacts.

5. **Risk Acceptance (RA) - Assess Phase:**
   - **Review Overall Risk Profile:** After completing the Measure phase, review the entire risk profile for your ISAE. This includes reassessing identified threats and their potential impacts as well as the effectiveness of controls.
   - **Accept or Reject Risks:** Based on this review, you decide whether to accept (keep) or reject (mitigate further) risks within acceptable limits. If you accept a risk, it means your organization is willing to live with the associated potential impacts and threats until conditions change.

6. **Continuous Monitoring (CM):**
   - **Monitor Risks:** Throughout the lifetime of your ISAE, continuously monitor for changes in threats, vulnerabilities, and implemented controls that could alter risk levels. This includes tracking security controls' effectiveness over time.
   - **Update the Risk Management Framework:** Periodically review, update, or reassess as needed using the entire RMF process to maintain a current understanding of your organization's information risks.

These phases form an iterative loop, often repeated annually for well-established systems and more frequently for new or rapidly changing systems. The goal is to continually improve risk management practices, ensuring that security controls remain effective in protecting your information assets over time.

How does NIST define the term 'least privilege', and why is it important?

2.252585355658084

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) defines "least privilege" as a security principle that restricts user or process access to a minimum level necessary for them to complete their intended tasks. This concept is central to NIST's Computer Security Resource Center (CSRC) guidelines, particularly in its publication titled "Security Requirements for Federal Information and Information Systems."

In the context of least privilege:

1. **Role-Based Access Control (RBAC):** NIST promotes RBAC, where permissions are granted based on a user's role within an organization. This means users only have access to data and actions relevant to their job functions. If a role does not require certain functionalities or specific data sets, those resources should be off-limits by default.

2. **Least Privilege Principle in Software:** For software applications, this principle is implemented by limiting the permissions granted to processes or services at runtime. This includes restricting file access, network connections, and execution privileges to only what's essential for the task at hand. 

3. **File System Permissions:** In file systems, least privilege translates to setting strict read and write permissions on folders and files based on user roles or the nature of operations (e.g., reading, writing, executing). 

The importance of the least privilege principle lies in its role as a fundamental security defense mechanism:

- **Reducing Attack Surface:** By limiting what each entity can do, you decrease the potential attack surface. The less access an actor has, the fewer points there are for malicious actors to exploit if they gain unauthorized entry into a system.

- **Enforcing Principle of Least Common Mechanism (PoLCM):** This principle states that similar tasks should not require similar privileges; hence, using least privilege helps enforce PoLCM.

- **Compliance and Accountability:** Implementing least privilege makes it easier to track user activities since they only have access to specific data or actions. This supports compliance with regulations like HIPAA, PCI DSS, or GDPR by demonstrating a stronger control over sensitive information.

- **Decreased Damage in Case of Breach:** Even if an attacker manages to breach a system due to a vulnerability in one component, the least privilege approach can limit the extent of damage because attackers' access is restricted unless specifically required for their task.

In summary, NIST's 'least privilege' definition emphasizes granting users or processes only the necessary permissions to perform tasks - a practice that significantly strengthens an organization's security posture by minimizing potential harm from breaches and ensuring better accountability over resources.

What guidance is given on quantum-resistant cryptography in SP 800-208?

The NIST Special Publication (SP) 800-208, "Guide for Developing Quantum-Safe Cryptographic Algorithms and Key Lengths," provides comprehensive guidance on the development of quantum-resistant cryptography. Here are some key points from this publication:

1. **Identifying Vulnerable Algorithms**: The guide starts by identifying existing symmetric and asymmetric cryptographic algorithms that could be vulnerable to attacks by quantum computers, known as post-quantum cryptography (PQC) threats. These include RSA, elliptic curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH), and the RSA and ECC key exchange methods based on these primitives.

2. **Evaluation Criteria**: The publication outlines several evaluation criteria for potential quantum-resistant algorithms:
   - **Security Bases**: Algorithms should provide security even when quantum computers become available, typically post-quantum security levels of 10 years (PQC-10).
   - **Efficiency**: They must be efficient in terms of computational resources and communication overhead.
   - **Key Sizes**: Quantum-resistant algorithms require larger key sizes to maintain the same level of security as their classical counterparts.

3. **Algorithm Selection**: Based on these criteria, SP 800-208 recommends a set of post-quantum cryptographic algorithms for different use cases:
   - **Symmetric Key Encrypted Algorithms**:
     - - FrodoKEM (a variant of the NIST PQC candidate KEM)
     - - Saber (another NIST PQC candidate)
   - **Public Key Encryption Algorithms**:
     - - New Hope, NTRU, and Frodo are recommended for key encapsulation.
     - - SPHINCS+ is suggested for digital signatures.

   - Symmetric: 256 bits (or higher)
   - Asymmetric (public keys): 3072 bits or higher (for PQC-10 level security).

5. **Hybrid Cryptosystems**: The publication suggests using hybrid cryptosystems that combine classical and post-quantum algorithms to ensure backward compatibility, resilience against known quantum attacks, and seamless transition when PQC becomes practical.

6. **Standardization Process**: SP 800-208 also discusses how these recommendations can be integrated into the NIST Standards for Post-Quantum Cryptography (NIST PQXL). This process ensures that chosen algorithms are thoroughly vetted and standardized for widespread use in various applications, including digital signatures, key exchange, and encryption.

In summary, SP 800-208 provides detailed guidance on identifying, evaluating, and recommending quantum-resistant cryptographic algorithms to maintain security standards against both classical and potential future quantum threats.
