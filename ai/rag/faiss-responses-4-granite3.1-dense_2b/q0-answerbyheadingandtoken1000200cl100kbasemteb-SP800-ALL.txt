# Direct fact recall


What is the primary purpose of NIST SP 800-53? 

4.178353553172201

The primary purpose of NIST SP 800-53, as stated in its various provisions across the provided documents, is to establish comprehensive security and privacy control baselines for federal information systems and organizations. These baselines serve as a foundation to meet protection needs and satisfy regulatory requirements from various acts, including FISMA (Federal Information Security Modernization Act), OMB A-130, PRIVACT (Privacy Act of 1974), and selected OMB policies.

In addition to these foundational controls, NIST SP 800-53 aims to:

1. Facilitate tailoring of the control baselines to better address specific organizational missions, business needs, stakeholder protection requirements, and risk assessments.
2. Provide a consolidated, outcome-based approach for information security and privacy controls within a single control catalog.
3. Integrate these controls with system engineering processes, software development practices, enterprise architecture principles, and other relevant domains.
4. Enable different communities of interest—such as systems engineers, security architects, developers, architects, and mission or business owners—to utilize the controls.
5. Continuously incorporate new, state-of-the-practice controls based on latest threat intelligence and cyber-attack data to enhance cyber resiliency, secure system design, and strengthen security and privacy governance and accountability.

In essence, NIST SP 800-53 serves as a roadmap for organizations to protect their information systems and manage the risks associated with them while also enabling the application of these protection measures across various professional disciplines.

According to SP 800-30, what is the definition of 'risk'?

4.2861506678164005

In the context of Special Publication 800-30 Rev. 1 by NIST, risk is defined as a measure that quantifies the extent to which an entity (such as an organization or its information systems) is threatened by potential circumstances or events. It is typically represented as a function of two primary components:

1. **Adverse impacts**: These are the consequences that could arise if the circumstance or event occurs, affecting various aspects like mission, functions, image/reputation, organizational assets, individuals, other organizations, and the Nation. They can include damage to an organization's reputation, financial loss due to inability to execute specific business processes, or resources spent on responding to information system incidents.

2. **Likelihood of occurrence**: This refers to how probable it is that the circumstance or event will take place.

The broad definition of risk encompasses a wide range of potential adverse impacts across different tiers in the risk management hierarchy, such as damage to an organization's image or reputation at Tier 1, loss of mission/business effectiveness due to data confidentiality loss at Tier 2, and expenditure on response to information system incidents at Tier 3.

Risk is typically expressed either as a single value or as a vector (i.e., multiple values), allowing for the assessment of different types of impacts independently. In risk communication, risks are generally grouped according to their adverse impacts and time frames in which these impacts may occur.

What are the steps in the risk assessment process dexcribed in SP 800-30?

4.597178713884205

The risk assessment process, as outlined in Special Publication 800-30 Rev. 1, consists of four main steps:

1. **Prepare for the Assessment:** In this initial step, organizations establish a context for the risk assessment by gathering and organizing information from the risk framing stage of their risk management process. This includes details about organizational policies and requirements related to conducting risk assessments, chosen assessment methodologies, procedures for selecting risk factors, the scope of the assessments, the rigor of analyses, and any other relevant formalities or requirements that ensure consistent and repeatable risk determinations across the organization (SP800-30 Rev. 1, Chapter 4).

2. **Conduct the Assessment:** During this step, organizations analyze threats and vulnerabilities, as well as impacts and likelihood, to produce a list of information security risks that can be prioritized by risk level. This process involves gathering essential information according to the assessment context established in the Prepare step (SP800-30 Rev. 1, Chapter 4).

3. **Communicate Assessment Results:** In this third step, organizations disseminate the findings of their risk assessments to key personnel within the organization. This communication is crucial for informing decision-making and aligning with the overall risk management strategy (SP800-30 Rev. 1, Chapter 4).

4. **Maintain the Assessment:** The final step in this process involves organizations consistently reviewing and updating their risk assessments over time to ensure that they remain relevant and effective. This ongoing maintenance is essential for tracking changes in the threat landscape and organizational risk tolerances (SP800-30 Rev. 1, Chapter 4).

Each of these steps has specific tasks listed under it, as described in Appendices D through I of SP800-30 Rev. 1. Additionally, supplementary guidance is provided for each task to assist organizations in conducting effective risk assessments (SP800-30 Rev. 1, Chapter 4).

Describe the phases of the risk management framework (RMF) as per SP 800-37.

4.841751290019602

The Risk Management Framework (RMF), as outlined in NIST Special Publication 800-37 Revision 2, consists of several phases that work together to provide a dynamic and flexible approach for managing security and privacy risks across diverse environments. These phases are not linear but can be reentered based on an assessment of risk. Here's a summary of the main steps in the RMF:

1. **Prepare (System Level)**: This is the initial phase, where organizations align their risk management roles with similar roles defined for Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC). The goal here is to establish the overall security and privacy protections required by the system or organization. Preparing involves identifying a proposed step and task entry into the RMF, performing gap analysis to ensure that the risk is acceptable, documenting decisions, notifying stakeholders, and seeking approval from an Authorizing Official (or other relevant decision maker).

2. **Plan**: In this phase, organizations develop detailed security plans for implementing controls at the system acquisition, development, or modification stages. This includes defining all applicable requirements to ensure that security is integrated into the design of the system.

3. **Implement**: Here, organizations put the planned controls into action according to their implementation plan. This step ensures that each control's required functionality and security attributes are met.

4. **Assess**: During this phase, organizations evaluate whether their implemented controls meet the requirements defined in the Plan and Policy documents. They conduct both configuration assessments (for systems) and risk assessments (for business operations and information systems). The purpose is to determine the overall effectiveness of the implemented security and privacy measures.

5. **Authorize**: In this critical final phase, an Authorizing Official or other relevant decision maker authorizes operation of the system after reviewing the results of the assessment. Once authorized, the system is considered ready for operational use while maintaining appropriate levels of security and privacy protections.

6. **Monitor**: Even after authorization, continuous monitoring is essential to ensure that systems remain secure and compliant with their established security requirements. Organizations must continually assess ongoing control effectiveness and make any necessary adjustments.

By following these phases, organizations can effectively manage security and privacy risks throughout the lifecycle of a system or organization. Each step in the RMF has a clear purpose statement, defined outcomes, and associated tasks to achieve those outcomes. This structured approach allows for flexibility in re-entering steps based on changing risk conditions and facilitates ongoing authorization and integration with SDLC processes.

How does NIST define the term 'least privilege', and why is it important?

3.6107431980781257

NIST defines 'least privilege' as a fundamental cybersecurity principle where each system component is granted just enough privileges to carry out its intended functions, but no more. This approach serves two primary purposes. Firstly, when these components fail, are corrupted, or are misused, the potential security impact is minimized due to their limited capabilities. Secondly, the analysis of such components becomes more straightforward because there's less complexity involved in understanding and managing their permissions.

The principle of least privilege reduces the attack surface of a system by limiting who has access to what resources. It ensures that no component or user can perform actions beyond what is necessary for its designated role. This concept permeates throughout all aspects of secure system design, from interfaces used to invoke functionalities to fine-grained decomposition of privileges in components like an audit mechanism, which could have different roles such as auditor manager, operator, and reviewer, each with distinct capabilities but no more.

In essence, NIST emphasizes least privilege as a critical cybersecurity strategy to maintain system integrity, mitigate potential damage from component failures or misuse, and simplify security management by controlling the permissions granted to each system component.

Why does NIST create these documents?

3.405046220868826

NIST develops these documents primarily under the Federal Information Security Management Act (FISMA) of 2002, which is Public Law 107-347. Their role, as outlined in these guidelines, is to establish and maintain consistent information security policies and procedures for federal information systems and organizations. This ensures that all agency operations and assets are adequately protected against cyber threats, while also respecting the specific security requirements of national security systems (as per OMB Circular A-130). By doing so, NIST aims to secure federal information, maintain its integrity, and prevent unauthorized access or disclosure.
