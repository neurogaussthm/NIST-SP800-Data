According to SP 800-30, what is the definition of 'risk'?

2.584975134115666

According to SP 800-30, risk is defined as a combination of two components: impact and likelihood. The risk level associated with identified threat events represents the degree to which an organization is threatened by those events. In general, the risk level is not higher than the impact level, and likelihood can serve to reduce risk below that impact level. However, when addressing organization-wide risk management issues with a large number of missions/business functions, processes, and supporting information systems, impact as an upper bound on risk may not hold. This means that multiple risks materializing, even if each risk is at the moderate level, could aggregate to a higher level of risk for organizations due to cumulative harm.

What are the steps in the risk assessment process dexcribed in SP 800-30?

4.994211324956268

The risk assessment process described in SP 800-30 consists of several key steps:

1. **IDENTIFY RISK MODEL AND ANALYTIC APPROACH (TASK 1-5)**: Organizations define one or more risk models, which include risk factors like threat, vulnerability, impact, likelihood, and predisposing conditions. They also select an analytic approach, specifying whether it's quantitative, qualitative, or semi-quantitative, and the type of analysis (threat-oriented, asset/impact-oriented, vulnerability-oriented). The organization identifies assessment scales for these risk factors, often using three scales: one qualitative and two semi-quantitative. These scales should be defined with organizationally meaningful examples and break points between bins in semi-quantitative approaches. Organizations may include or tailor from the appendices to develop their assessment scales.

2. **IDENTIFY KEY RISK FACTORS FOR MONITORING (TASK 4)**: Organizations determine which risk factors are crucial for ongoing monitoring and prioritize them based on their potential impacts, current risks, or importance of core missions/business functions.

3. **DEFINE MONITORING FREQUENCY AND CIRCUMSTANCES FOR RISK ASSESSMENT UPDATE (TASK 4-2)**: Organizations establish the frequency and criteria for updating risk assessments due to significant changes in risk factors, such as new threats, vulnerabilities, predisposing conditions, undesirable consequences, or affected assets.

4. **RECONFIRM PURPOSE, SCOPE, AND ASSUMPTIONS OF THE RISK ASSESSMENT (TASK 4-1)**: Organizations review and reaffirm the purpose of the risk assessment, its scope, and associated assumptions to ensure they remain valid for subsequent assessments.

5. **CONDUCT APPROPRIATE RISK ASSESSMENT TASKS (AS NEEDED)**: Based on identified key factors and updated purpose, scope, and assumptions, organizations perform the necessary tasks from Task 2-1 through Task 2-4, which include:
   - Identifying threats, vulnerabilities, predisposing conditions, undesirable consequences, or affected assets.
   - Assessing threat source characteristics (capability, intent, targeting range of effects), likelihoods, and impacts for each identified factor.

6. **COMBINE RISK FACTOR VALUES USING ASSEMBLED ALGORITHMS** (TASK 2-4): If using an organization-specific risk model with algorithms for combining values based on relative weights applied to risk factors, organizations apply these formulas or rules during this step.

7. **COMMUNICATE RISK ASSESSMENT RESULTS** (TASK 5-3 and BEYOND): Organizations disseminate the outcomes of subsequent risk assessments across all risk management tiers to ensure responsible officials have access to critical information for ongoing risk-based decisions. This communication typically includes:
   - Identified new or changed risk factors.
   - Assessments of threat source characteristics, likelihoods, and impacts.

These steps emphasize the iterative nature of the risk assessment process, ensuring that organizations maintain an up-to-date understanding of their risk landscape to make informed decisions across all tiers.

Describe the phases of the risk management framework (RMF) as per SP 800-37.

5.293851810973138

The risk management framework (RMF), as described in SP 800-37, consists of several phases designed to provide a structured and comprehensive approach to managing information system-related security and privacy risks at various organizational levels. The RMF operates at three primary levels: the organization level, mission/business process level, and information system level (see Figure 1 in [SP 800-37]). Here are the phases of the RMF, as outlined in SP 800-37:

1. **Prepare:** This initial phase focuses on preparing the organization for executing the RMF. It includes activities that go beyond managing security and privacy risk associated with specific systems, such as understanding mission or business objectives, modernization initiatives, enterprise architecture, and resource allocation. The primary goals of this phase are to facilitate communication between stakeholders and develop an organizationally-tailored control baseline for system owners, thereby reducing the workload on individual system owners and the overall cost of system development and asset protection.

2. **Plan:** In this phase, organizations define their security and privacy risk management plans at the mission/business process level. The plan includes defining authorization boundaries, establishing security and privacy controls, identifying responsible parties for implementing and maintaining those controls, and setting up processes for assessing, monitoring, and evaluating the effectiveness of these controls throughout the system lifecycle.

3. **Implement:** During this phase, organizations implement the identified security and privacy controls as outlined in their approved plans from the Plan phase. This includes developing, testing, integrating, and deploying technical security mechanisms at various stages of the system life cycle (e.g., development, acquisition, operational maintenance).

4. **Assess:** In this evaluation phase, organizations determine whether their implemented controls effectively implement their intended protection policies. Assessments include both configuration reviews to verify control configurations as well as testing to ensure that the controls operate correctly under various conditions and in response to actual attacks or threats. The goal of assessment is to identify any gaps between planned and actual security and privacy features, allowing organizations to remediate issues and improve their overall risk posture.

5. **Authorize:** Upon completing assessments, organizations review the results and determine if the implemented controls satisfy their security and privacy requirements for specific systems or mission/business processes. If so, authorization is granted; otherwise, further action such as designating appropriate levels of safeguards (e.g., high, moderate, or low) may be necessary to allow operations.

6. **Monitor:** After authorizing systems or processes, organizations monitor them continuously for ongoing security and privacy controls' effectiveness. This includes tracking system changes, detecting potential intrusions or anomalies, analyzing log data, and performing periodic reassessments as part of the Continual Diagnostics process (CDP). Monitoring also involves taking corrective actions based on detected issues to maintain a secure environment throughout the system lifecycle.

7. **Communicate:** Organizations continuously communicate security and privacy information between stakeholders at different levels, ensuring they are informed about risks, vulnerabilities, and implemented controls' status. This two-way communication helps maintain awareness and enables quick response to changing risk landscapes or system conditions.

8. **Perform ongoing operations:** In this phase, organizations perform necessary tasks to sustain the RMF's lifecycle over time. Such activities may include updating plans based on new risks, reviewing and improving controls as needed, conducting periodic reassessments, or making adjustments in response to lessons learned from monitoring and other feedback mechanisms.

By following these phases of the RMF, organizations can ensure a systematic, organized approach to managing security and privacy risk throughout their information technology infrastructure.

How does NIST define the term 'least privilege', and why is it important?

3.5138343437574804

NIST defines "least privilege" as a security principle that programs or users should operate with only the specific privileges required to perform their intended tasks. This concept encourages running code at the lowest possible level of permission, minimizing potential damage in case of exploitation or misuse. By adhering to this principle, organizations can:

1. Reduce attack surfaces: Limiting access and permissions prevents unauthorized users from gaining excessive control over a system, making it harder for malware and other malicious actors to execute harmful operations.
2. Enforce least privilege at the user level: Assign users to rights-limited groups instead of full administrator accounts to prevent the spread of malware and ensure only authorized applications run on their systems.
3. Facilitate granular permission control: Allowing fine-grained permission settings for incoming mobile code (e.g., Java applets) enables users to customize access levels, further reducing potential security risks.
4. Mitigate insider threats and unauthorized activities: Restrict the impact of compromised accounts by limiting privileges, making it harder for malicious actors to cause extensive damage or move laterally within a network.

The importance of adhering to least privilege lies in its ability to build robust security defenses while reducing potential attack surfaces and minimizing damage from successful breaches. By implementing this principle consistently across systems, software applications, and user accounts, organizations can better protect themselves against various cyber threats and maintain the integrity of their digital assets.

5. **Security trade-offs**: Systems engineers consider the costs and benefits of security features, making informed decisions during engineering trades and risk management. This ensures a balance between security requirements and other system constraints, such as performance, cost, or operational feasibility.

6. **Security analyses and interpretation**: Security professionals perform independent security analyses to support decision-making for engineering trades and risk management. 7. **Leveraging security specialties and contributions**: Security engineers collaborate with various specialties, including computer security, communications security, physical security, information and software assurance, and technology domains like biometrics and cryptography. In summary, SP 800-160 underscores that systems engineering principles for secure design involve a holistic approach encompassing collaboration, risk assessment, quantifying security requirements, considering trade-offs, performing analyses, and leveraging diverse expertise to meet stakeholder objectives and protect the system from adversity.

What guidance is given on quantum-resistant cryptography in SP 800-208?

4.323795807082206

In NIST Special Publication (SP) 800-208, Quantum-Safe Cryptographic Algorithms (QCA), the following guidance is provided regarding quantum-resistant cryptography:

1. **Post-Quantum Algorithms**: SP 800-208 introduces a set of post-quantum algorithms for use in Federal Information Processing Standards (FIPS) and Special Publications (SPs). These algorithms are designed to resist attacks by both classical and quantum computers, ensuring long-term security.

2. **Algorithm Selection**: The publication recommends the use of NIST's post-quantum key encapsulation mechanisms (KEMs) and digital signature algorithms for FIPS 140-2 Level 3 physical security modules (PSMs). Specifically, it suggests using the following:
   - NIST P-256 KEM for key generation, key transport, and key derivation.
   - NIST P-384 KEM for key generation, key transport, and key derivation.
   - NIST X25519 or X464 digital signature algorithm for digital signatures.

3. **Migration Plan**: Federal departments and agencies are advised to consider potential cryptographic key length migrations as part of their moderate to long-term cryptographic transition and modernization plans, with an emphasis on planning and investing in a future migration to post-quantum algorithms once foundational standards supporting their use have been adopted.

4. **Post-Quantum Algorithms**: SP 800-208 outlines that post-quantum algorithms will be specified in future revisions of NIST publications, such as when the PSIRQ (Post-Quantum Standardization) process has developed and agreed upon quantum-resistant candidates.

What is the difference between ISO 27001 and NIST SP 800-53?

4.693391545210034

ISO 27001 and NIST Special Publication (SP) 800-53 are both widely recognized frameworks for information security management systems (ISMS), but they differ in their scope, structure, and emphasis. Here's a comparison of the two:

1. **Scope**:
   - ISO 27001 is an international standard published by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) that provides requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an ISMS. It focuses on information security management in general and does not include specific technical controls like NIST SP 800-53.
   - NIST SP 800-53 is a U.S. government publication that offers detailed technical guidelines for the development, implementation, and continuous monitoring of control systems in federal information systems and organizations. It provides a comprehensive set of security and privacy controls based on risk assessment principles.

2. **Structure**:
   - ISO 27001 follows a more general structure with clauses addressing various aspects of ISMS, such as scope, context, leadership, risk treatment, measures, implementation, internal audits, management review, and continual improvement.
   - NIST SP 800-53 is structured into eight volumes that cover technical controls for protecting information systems. Each volume focuses on specific control objectives, such as access control (volume 1), physical and environmental protection (volume 2), and system development, configuration management, and maintenance (volume 7).

3. **Emphasis**:
   - ISO 27001 emphasizes the establishment of a risk-based approach to managing sensitive information by organizations. It encourages customers and stakeholders' involvement in ISMS implementation.
   - NIST SP 800-53 focuses on providing specific technical controls, procedures, and guidelines for protecting government information systems and privacy rights from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction.

4. **Certification**:
   - ISO 27001 allows organizations to pursue certification (ISO/IEC 17025-based) for demonstrating compliance with the standard's requirements.
   - NIST SP 800-53 does not support certification in a formal sense, but its extensive control set serves as the basis for security and privacy risk assessments, design, implementation, monitoring, and continuous improvement efforts within U.S. government agencies and other organizations.

In summary, while both ISO 27001 and NIST SP 800-53 aim to provide a solid foundation for managing information security risks, they differ in their focus areas: ISO 27001 takes a broader approach to ISMS management, including customer involvement and risk treatment; whereas NIST SP 800-53 concentrates on detailed technical controls for protecting U.S. government systems and privacy rights.

How long does it take to follow RMF?

4.065518653020263

The time required to fully execute the Risk Management Framework (RMF) depends on several factors, including the size and complexity of the information system, available resources, and the organization's maturity in implementing security and privacy practices. It is challenging to provide an exact duration as it can vary from a few months to over a year for extensive systems.

Here is a rough breakdown of the time allocated to each step in the RMF, based on NIST guidelines:

1. Preparatory step (Level 0): Approximately one to four weeks. This involves organizational assessment and preparation for risk management activities.
2. Level 1: A few days to a couple of weeks. This level focuses on identifying the scope of information systems, defining their security requirements, and selecting initial controls based on risk decisions made at higher levels (Levels 0 and 2).
3. Levels 2 and 3: Typically around two to four months each for most organizations due to the extensive planning required in identifying and assessing security and privacy risks, selecting appropriate controls, implementing them, and authorizing systems.
4. Levels 4, 5, and 6 (Continuous Monitoring): These steps are ongoing, and the time allocated depends on how frequently an organization needs to monitor its information systems for changes or new risks. Organizations typically dedicate a small fraction of their annual budget and dedicated resources toward this step.

In summary, it takes several months to over a year for most organizations to execute the RMF completely, depending on factors like system size, complexity, available resources, and the organization's existing security posture.

Why does NIST create these documents?

3.1386701483279467

NIST creates these guidelines (such as SP800-122) to fulfill its statutory responsibilities under the Federal Information Security Management Act (FISMA) of 2002, specifically to provide standards and guidelines for ensuring adequate information security for all agency operations and assets. These documents serve as practical resources for organizations within the Federal government, including nongovernmental entities using them on a voluntary basis. NIST aims to help these groups understand and implement best practices in protecting sensitive data like personally identifiable information (PII) while adhering to various privacy laws and regulations. The documents offer context-based guidance, safeguards recommendations, and incident response planning tailored to meet specific organizational needs. By creating these resources, NIST seeks to strengthen the overall information security posture of Federal agencies and those organizations working with them.
