How does NIST define the term 'least privilege', and why is it important?

3.656413014046848

The NIST SP 800 series defines the term "least privilege" as a security design principle that states each component of an information system should be allocated sufficient privileges to accomplish its specified functions, but no more. This means that the scope of a component's actions should be limited to what is necessary for its intended function.

In other words, the principle of least privilege ensures that users and system processes do not have access to too much power or resources, which can increase the attack surface of the system and reduce security.

The importance of least privilege lies in its ability to minimize the impact of a failure, corruption, or misuse of the system component. By limiting the scope of an individual's privileges, organizations can simplify their security analysis and reduce the likelihood of unauthorized access or operations on the system.

This principle is reflected in all aspects of secure system design, from personnel with responsibilities for defining least privileges to system administrators who must implement and maintain this policy.

It's worth noting that NIST encourages organizations to consider creating additional processes, roles, and system accounts to achieve the principles of least privilege. This helps to reduce the attack surface of the system and improves overall security posture.

In summary, the term "least privilege" is defined as a security design principle that limits the scope of an individual's privileges to what is necessary for their intended function, minimizing the impact of a failure, corruption, or misuse of the system component.
