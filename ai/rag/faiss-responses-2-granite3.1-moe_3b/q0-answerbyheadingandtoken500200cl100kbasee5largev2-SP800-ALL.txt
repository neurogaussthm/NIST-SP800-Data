# Direct fact recall


What is the primary purpose of NIST SP 800-53? 

4.175200347788632

The primary purpose of NIST SP 800-53 is to provide a comprehensive set of security and privacy control baselines for various computing platforms, including general-purpose systems, cyber-physical systems, cloud-based systems, mobile devices, and industrial control systems. This publication aims to offer flexibility in tailoring these baselines while ensuring consistency in conducting control assessments across different organizations. By removing control baselines and tailoring guidance from SP 800-53A, NIST has developed SP 800-53B specifically for information systems and organizations, offering a flexible starting point for assessing security and privacy controls within these environments. This resource serves as an initial step in the process of defining procedures for evaluating the security and privacy controls in systems and organizations, promoting consistent application and customization based on specific organizational policies, threat/vulnerability information, operational considerations, system dependencies, and risk tolerance levels.

According to SP 800-30, what is the definition of 'risk'?

4.58115500304848

According to NIST SP 800-30 Rev. 1, risk is defined as a function of the likelihood of a threat event's occurrence and potential adverse impact should the event occur. This encompasses various types of adverse impacts at different tiers in the risk management hierarchy, including financial loss (Tier 1), mission/business process inability to execute (Tier 2), or response-related resources expenditure (Tier 3). The definition also allows for risk representation as a single value or vector, where multiple types of adverse impacts can be assessed separately. For effective communication of risks, they are typically grouped by the nature and time frames of these potential impacts. This broad definition ensures that risk is not limited to traditional information system-based threats but can also include broader implications such as damage to organizational reputation or loss of mission/business effectiveness due to data confidentiality breaches.

How does NIST SP 800-61 relate to NIST SP 800-137 in terms of continuous monitoring and incident response?

5.399486017879099

NIST SP 800-61, Revision 2, provides guidelines for a general approach to incident handling, including the use of simulated incidents and exercise scenarios as mentioned. Simultaneously, NIST SP 800-137 focuses on continuous monitoring in cybersecurity environments.

While both SP 800-61 and SP 800-137 are crucial for effective cybersecurity management, they address different aspects of incident handling and security practices. 

SP 800-61 primarily outlines the process of incident response, including steps to preserve evidence (as seen in Appendix A), which is essential for maintaining a forensically sound investigation. It emphasizes that initial system snapshots are often more effective than post-incident actions for identifying and understanding an incident's nature and source.

On the other hand, SP 800-137 is about continuous monitoring, providing recommendations on how to maintain and analyze security alerts generated by various systems in real-time. This standard helps organizations ensure their cybersecurity posture remains robust without unnecessary interruptions or overreactions.

In summary, NIST SP 800-61 pertains more to incident response procedures and evidence preservation during an event, whereas SP 800-137 focuses on continuous monitoring to provide real-time insight into potential threats. Both are integral parts of a comprehensive cybersecurity strategy as outlined in the NIST SP 800 series.

# Procedural/Sequential questions


What are the steps in the risk assessment process dexcribed in SP 800-30?

4.885144378058612

The steps in the risk assessment process, as described in NIST Special Publication 800-30 (Rev. 1), include:

1. **Preparing for the Assessment**: This involves setting up the necessary environment and resources to conduct a comprehensive risk assessment.

2. **Conducting the Assessment**: This step entails evaluating the system or organization against identified risk criteria, identifying potential vulnerabilities, threats, and impacts. The results of this assessment are then documented and analyzed.

3. **Communicating the Assessment Results**: This is a critical step where decision-makers across the organization receive necessary information to guide their decisions regarding risks. It involves sharing risk assessment results along with relevant security assessments conducted at various stages of the system development life cycle, detailing the type of assessments, frequency, level of rigor applied, and types/numbers of objects assessed.

4. **Maintaining the Assessment**: This step ensures that the risk management process is continuously updated to reflect changes in the environment or systems. It involves periodic reassessments to verify if risks have increased beyond acceptable levels (exceeding organizational risk tolerance) and appropriate actions are taken.

These steps ensure a systematic approach to identifying, evaluating, and managing risks within an organization's information systems.

Describe the phases of the risk management framework (RMF) as per SP 800-37.

6.968707506079227

The Risk Management Framework (RMF), as outlined in NIST Special Publication 800-37 Revision 2, consists of several key phases that guide organizations through the management of security and privacy risks at various levels within their information systems and organizational structures. Here are the main steps involved:

1. **Prepare:** This initial phase involves preparing for RMF application by ensuring that necessary policies, standards, and procedures are in place. It also includes establishing the context of risk management, which provides the foundation for further activities.

2. **Categorize:** In this step, information systems and organizational entities (such as individuals or organizations) within the system boundaries are categorized based on their sensitivity and associated risks. This categorization helps in determining appropriate security controls to be implemented at each level of risk.

3. **Select Controls:** Based on the categorization, relevant security controls are selected from a list of approved controls provided by SP 800-37 Rev. 2. These controls could include those outlined in SP 800-64 (Cryptographic Management), SP 800-160 v1 (Stakeholder Needs and Requirements Definition Process), and others like SP 800-161 for multi-tiered risk management, and IR 8179.

4. **Implement Controls:** The selected controls are then implemented across the relevant information systems or organizations. This could involve activities such as configuring systems to use approved cryptographic modules, setting up access controls based on categorization, etc.

5. **Assess Risk:** After implementation, risk is reassessed using the new security controls in place. The goal is to ensure that the selected and implemented controls are effective in mitigating identified risks.

6. **Authorize Systems and Controls:** Based on the risk assessment, system authorizations are updated to reflect changes made by these controls. This process ensures ongoing authorization for information systems and their associated security controls.

7. **Monitor:** Continuous monitoring is crucial throughout all RMF phases. It involves tracking system statuses, detecting any unauthorized activities, responding to detected threats or vulnerabilities, recovering from potential breaches, and reassessing risks to ensure the effectiveness of implemented controls.

8. **Review and Adjust:** The process loops back to the Prepare phase periodically (or continuously) for review and adjustment based on changes in risk, technological advancements, or organizational needs. This ongoing cycle ensures that the RMF remains effective in managing risks associated with information systems over time.

# Policy interpretation/Coneceptual paraphrasing


How does NIST define the term 'least privilege', and why is it important?

5.35939631704241

NIST defines the principle of 'Least Privilege' (as per SP800-53 Rev. 5) as each system component being granted sufficient privileges to perform its intended functions without more. This principle limits the scope of a component's actions, thereby minimizing the potential security impact of any failure, corruption, or misuse. The security analysis of the component is also simplified due to this limitation.

Least privilege is crucial for maintaining robust cybersecurity for several reasons:

1. **Reduced Attack Surface**: By granting only the necessary privileges, the potential damage an attacker can cause from a single point (a compromised system) is significantly reduced.

2. **Simplified Security Management**: The security posture of each component becomes more manageable and easier to assess because its actions are confined within defined boundaries.

3. **Mitigation of Errors or Mistakes**: Even if a user or process accidentally executes code with excessive privileges, the damage they can do is limited by least privilege, as opposed to having unrestricted access to resources.

4. **Compliance and Best Practices**: Implementing least privilege aligns with various cybersecurity best practices and regulatory requirements, such as NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5, which emphasizes the importance of this principle in mitigating risks across a range of systems.

In essence, 'Least Privilege' is not just a technical measure, but also an overarching strategy that enhances both security and manageability within cybersecurity frameworks.

Explain the purpose of security categorization according to NIST.

5.787246187217534

Security categorization, as outlined in various NIST publications such as SP800-37 Rev. 2, SP800-144, SP800-60 Vol. 1 Rev. 1, and SP800-70 Rev. 4, serves a crucial role in the certification and accreditation (C&A) process for federal information systems. Its primary purpose is to establish a standardized methodology for categorizing information and information systems based on their risk levels.

This categorization provides several key benefits:

1. **Risk-Based Security**: It ensures that an adequate level of security is provided, which is commensurate with the system's risk. The categorization helps in assigning appropriate security controls to manage and mitigate identified risks effectively.

2. **Compliance Assistance**: By establishing clear levels of rigor for certification and assurance testing, it guides organizations on how much effort and time are required for C&A activities. This transparency aids in compliance with regulatory requirements like FIPS 200.

3. **Support for Other Activities**: The categorization lays the foundation for other critical activities such as privacy impact analysis, critical infrastructure protection (CIP), and security control selection. It helps determine the level of effort and duration needed for these related tasks.

4. **Continuous Improvement**: Security categories are revisited at least every three years or after significant changes in the system or its supporting business lines. This frequent review allows for an assessment of whether the current categorization still accurately reflects the system's risk, necessitating potential re-categorization to maintain a robust security posture.

In essence, NIST's security categorization is not just about assigning levels of rigor; it's about ensuring that systems are appropriately secured based on their unique risks and supporting them throughout their lifecycle through continuous assessment and adjustment.

What does SP 800-160 say about systems engineering principles for secure design?

6.280892112292349

NIST Special Publication (SP) 800-160, Volume 2, provides guidance on systems security engineering with a strong emphasis on protection against asset loss. It presents cyber resiliency engineering as an emerging specialty discipline within the broader context of secure design principles. The publication suggests that many of these cyber resiliency design principles are derived from and can be aligned with those found in security, resilience engineering, or both (as indicated by Ricci14).

The cyber resiliency design principles, as outlined in SP 800-160 Vol. 2 Rev. 1, encompass a strategic approach that guides the direction of system life cycle processes throughout [SP 800-160 v1]. This strategic alignment can be observed in systems engineering artifacts like risk management processes (as per SP 800-39) and the Risk Management Framework for Information Systems and Organizations (as detailed in SP 800-37).

Additionally, structural cyber resiliency design principles are characterized as directly affecting the architecture and design of systems or system elements. These can be reflected in various security-related systems engineering artifacts, including those from ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288:2015 (Systems and software engineering—Systems life cycle processes) and NIST SP 800-37 (Risk Management Framework for Information Systems and Organizations).

This comprehensive approach to secure design in SP 800-160 Vol. 2 Rev. 1 allows organizations to select, adapt, and utilize cyber resiliency constructs—such as goals, objectives, techniques, approaches, and design principles—to engineer more trustworthy systems that can anticipate, withstand, recover from, and adapt to adverse conditions, stresses, attacks, or compromises utilizing or enabled by cyber resources.

What guidance is given on quantum-resistant cryptography in SP 800-208?

5.007456792052835

In the NIST SP 800 series, specifically in SP 800-57 Revision 2, there's no dedicated section discussing "Quantum-Resistant Cryptography." However, NIST continues to evolve its guidelines and recommendations as technology advances. 

NIST has been actively working on quantum-resistant cryptographic methods due to the potential threats posed by quantum computing. For instance, SP 800-56A (Recommendation for Pair-Wise Key Establishment Schemes Using Discrete Logarithm Cryptography) and SP 800-56B (Recommendation for Pair-Wise Key Establishment Schemes Using Integer Factorization Cryptography) have been revised to include post-quantum cryptographic algorithms.

While SP 800-57 Part 1, Recommendation for Key Management, doesn't specifically address quantum resistant cryptography, the broader guidelines in this series (SP 800-56A, SP 800-56B, and others) encourage the use of post-quantum algorithms where applicable. 

Moreover, NIST's upcoming publication "SP 800-380: Standard for Public Key Cryptography" will likely provide more detailed guidance on quantum resistant cryptographic methods. 

As always, it's recommended to stay updated with the latest NIST publications as they significantly influence cybersecurity policy and standards.

Is continuous monitoring a step in the risk assessment process according to SP 800-30?

6.546773016918451

Yes, according to the NIST Special Publication 800-30 Rev. 1, continuous monitoring is indeed considered a step within the risk assessment process. It involves evaluating changes in security controls, information systems, and compliance with federal laws, regulations, directives, policies, standards, and guidance. This ongoing evaluation helps organizations understand if their identified risks are still valid (whether they have exceeded organizational risk tolerance) and guides them on appropriate responses to these risks.

In this context:
- Step 4 is dedicated to maintaining the assessment. The primary objective here is to ensure that the specific knowledge of the risks an organization faces remains current, as it informs and guides risk management decisions and responses.
- Continuous monitoring plays a crucial role in achieving this goal by regularly assessing changes (i) in the effectiveness of risk responses; (ii) to information systems and operations that may affect those risks; and (iii) compliance with various requirements, including legislation and directives. By comparing these ongoing evaluations against previous ones, organizations can ascertain if their risk management strategy needs adjustment, whether it's in acquisition decisions, authorization of information systems, or connection decisions, to effectively respond to the evolving risks they face.

This continuous monitoring is intertwined with other steps in the risk assessment process (preparation, conducting, communicating, and maintaining) and supports an overall risk management strategy that incorporates lessons learned from risk assessments and improves responses over time.

Does SP 800-171 recommend two-factor authentication for all users?

4.827183485031128

Based on the provided context from NIST SP 800-171 Rev. 3, multi-factor authentication (MFA) is indeed recommended and it requires the use of at least two different factors to achieve authentication. This requirement applies to user accounts, as per SP 800-171A [84]. The specific authentication factors mentioned in MFA include something you know (e.g., a personal identification number [PIN]), something you have (e.g., a physical authenticator such as a cryptographic private key), or something you are (e.g., a biometric). However, it's important to note that not all families from SP 800-53 are included in SP 800-171 due to tailoring criteria. For instance, the PII Processing and Transparency (PT) family is not included as personally identifiable information (PII) is a category of Confidentiality-Protecting Information (CUI). Similarly, families like Program Management (PM) and Contingency Planning (CP) are also not included due to their non-applicability in this context.

# Out of scope/control


What is the difference between ISO 27001 and NIST SP 800-53?

5.622126483358443

ISO 27001 and NIST SP 800-53 are both widely recognized frameworks for information security management, but they have distinct differences in scope, approach, and implementation.

ISO 27001 is an international standard that outlines a range of requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an Information Security Management System (ISMS). It provides a framework for creating policies, defining roles and responsibilities, identifying information security risks, implementing appropriate controls to mitigate those risks, monitoring the ISMS's effectiveness, and reporting on its performance. ISO 27001 is based on international best practices and does not specifically tailor to any particular type of computing platform or organization.

On the other hand, NIST SP 800-53 (Rev. 5) provides security control baselines for federal information systems and organizations, as well as guidance for tailoring those baselines and developing overlays that support the security and privacy requirements of various stakeholders. It's particularly focused on federal systems but also includes guidelines for non-federal entities like private sector organizations. SP 800-53 offers a more detailed set of controls, organized around six categories: Identification and Authentication, Access Control, Awareness and Training, Information Systems Acquisition, Construction, and Maintenance, Physical and Environmental Protection, and Supply Chain Security.

In essence, while ISO 27001 offers a broad framework for establishing an ISMS across various industries and sectors, NIST SP 800-53 (Rev. 5) provides detailed security control baselines specific to federal information systems and organizations, along with guidance on tailoring these controls and developing additional overlays as needed by different stakeholders.
