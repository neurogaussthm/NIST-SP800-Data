How does NIST define the term 'least privilege', and why is it important?

2.6433572312816978

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) defines "least privilege" as a fundamental principle in access control, which states that an individual should have only the privileges and access necessary to perform their job functions.

Here's a summary:

**Definition:** Least privilege is a security concept where an individual or system has the minimum required access rights to perform a specific function, while avoiding the need for elevated privileges beyond what is strictly necessary.

**Why it's important:**

1. **Security risk reduction**: By limiting the scope of access to a particular function, least privilege helps reduce the attack surface and decreases the risk of unauthorized access or breaches.
2. **Data protection**: Least privilege ensures that sensitive data is not accessed by users who do not need it for their job functions.
3. **Operational efficiency**: Access control based on least privilege simplifies the management of user accounts and privileges, reducing administrative overhead.

To implement least privilege, organizations should consider the following best practices:

1. **Define clear access rights**: Establish explicit access rights for each user or system component that requires a specific level of access.
2. **Use role-based access control (RBAC)**: Assign users to roles based on their job functions and assign access rights accordingly.
3. **Limit privileges**: Restrict the scope of privileges to only those required for performing job functions.
4. **Monitor and review access**: Regularly review access logs and user permissions to ensure that least privilege is being followed.

By adopting a least privilege approach, organizations can reduce security risks, improve data protection, and enhance operational efficiency.

3. **Ensure consistency**: The documents ensure that control names, descriptions, and related resources are consistent across different domains, sectors, and applications.
4. **Maintain stability**: By not renumbering withdrawn controls, NIST maintains a historical record of controls that were previously withdrawn or superseded by newer standards.

Some examples of NIST documents include:

* Vulnerability naming schemes (e.g., CVE and CCE)
* Security and privacy policies
* Information security frameworks
* Data protection guidelines
* Access control best practices

Overall, the goal is to provide a comprehensive set of resources for organizations to manage information security, while promoting consistency, stability, and effective use of controls across various contexts.
