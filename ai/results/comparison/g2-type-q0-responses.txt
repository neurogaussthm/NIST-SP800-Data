"""The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) defines "least privilege" as an information security concept that restricts users' access to system resources, files, or capabilities only to what is necessary for their current tasks. This means each user should have the minimum level of permission required to perform job functions effectively while preventing unauthorized access, data breaches, and potential damage from insider threats.

According to NIST Special Publication 82 (SP 82), "Guidelines for Program Managers on Security Controls Integration," there are three key principles of least privilege:

1. **Minimal Privilege**: Users should be given only the rights needed to accomplish their job responsibilities, no more. This minimizes the potential damage an attacker could cause if a user's credentials were compromised.

2. **Least Privilege Principle in Granting Authority**: When granting permissions or access rights, it is essential to apply this principle rigorously. This means avoiding broad, default permissions and instead issuing granular, task-based access.

3. **No Vacuums of Privilege**: There should be no period where a user has more privileges than they need because their current work might lead to other tasks requiring additional rights. Continuous authorization is vital here.

Least privilege is crucial for several reasons:

- **Enhanced Security**: By limiting users' access, you reduce the attack surface and minimize opportunities for cybercriminals to exploit vulnerabilities or perform unauthorized actions. 

- **Compliance with Regulations**: Many regulatory frameworks (e.g., HIPAA, PCI DSS, GDPR) mandate least privilege principles as part of their security requirements. Adhering to these standards helps organizations avoid hefty fines and maintain their reputation for robust data protection.

- **Accountability and Audit Trails**: Implementing the principle of least privilege makes it easier to track who has accessed what resources and when, facilitating accountability and simplifying compliance with audit requirements.

- **Reduced Impact of Insider Threats**: Even if a user's credentials are compromised or misused by an insider, limiting their access reduces the damage they can cause since they won't have broader permissions that could grant unrestricted data manipulation or deletion capabilities.

In summary, NIST's definition of least privilege as a core security concept emphasizes efficient control over user access rights and resources to prevent both external and internal threats while ensuring organizational compliance with regulations designed to protect sensitive information."""
